Asteroid

[visited asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_minor_planets_and_comets_visited_by_spacecraft) illustrating their differences: (top row) [433 Eros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/433_Eros) and [243 Ida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/243_Ida) with its moon [Dactyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dactyl_(moon)) , (bottom row) [1 Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet) )and [101955 Bennu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/101955_Bennu) . Sizes are not to scale.

An asteroid is a [minor planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_planet)—an object larger than a [meteoroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoroid) (thus 1 meter or larger) that is neither a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet) nor an identified [comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet)—that orbits within the [inner Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System#Inner_Solar_System) or is co-orbital with [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter) ([Trojan asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_asteroid)). Asteroids are rocky, metallic, or icy bodies with no atmosphere, and are broadly classified into [C-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-type_asteroid) ([carbonaceous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon)), [M-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-type_asteroid) ([metallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal)), or [S-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-type_asteroid) ([silicaceous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silica)).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-DeMeoCarry2013-1) The size and shape of asteroids vary significantly, ranging from small rubble piles under a kilometer across to [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)), a [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet) almost 1000 km in diameter. A body is classified as a [comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet), not an asteroid, if it shows a coma (tail) when warmed by solar radiation, although recent observations suggest a continuum between these types of bodies.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:0-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:1-3)

Of the roughly one million known asteroids,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-4) the greatest number are located between the orbits of [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars) and [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter), approximately 2 to 4 [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit) (AU) from the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun), in a region known as the main [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt). The total mass of all the asteroids combined is only 3% that of [Earth's Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon). The majority of main belt asteroids follow slightly elliptical, stable orbits, revolving in the same direction as the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) and taking from three to six years to complete a full [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit) around the Sun.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-press-kit-5)

Asteroids have been observed from Earth since their discovery in 1801. The first close-up observation of an asteroid was made by the [Galileo spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)). Several dedicated missions to asteroids were subsequently launched by [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA) and [JAXA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JAXA), with plans for other missions in progress. NASA's [NEAR Shoemaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NEAR_Shoemaker) studied [Eros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/433_Eros), and [Dawn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawn_(spacecraft)) observed [Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta) and [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_Ceres). JAXA's missions [Hayabusa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayabusa) and [Hayabusa2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayabusa2) studied and returned samples of [Itokawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/25143_Itokawa) and [Ryugu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/162173_Ryugu), respectively. [OSIRIS-REx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OSIRIS-REx) studied [Bennu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/101955_Bennu), collecting a sample in 2020 which was delivered back to Earth in 2023. NASA's [Lucy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucy_(spacecraft)), launched in 2021, is tasked with studying ten different asteroids, two from the [main belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt) and eight [Jupiter trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan). NASA's [Double Asteroid Redirection Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Asteroid_Redirection_Test) (DART), launched in 2021, was intentionally crashed into the non-threatening asteroid [Dimorphos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimorphos) in September 2022 as a test of [planetary defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_impact_avoidance) technology. NASA's [Psyche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psyche_(spacecraft)), launched October 2023, aims to study the metallic [asteroid Psyche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16_Psyche). ESA's [Hera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hera_(space_mission)), launched in October 2024, is intended to study the results of the DART impact. CNSA's [Tianwen-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianwen-2) was launched in May 2025,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-auto-6) to explore the [co-orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital) near-Earth asteroid [469219 Kamoʻoalewa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/469219_Kamoʻoalewa) and the [active asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_asteroid)[311P/PanSTARRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/311P/PanSTARRS) and collecting samples of the regolith of Kamo'oalewa.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nature20190430-7)

Near-Earth asteroids have the potential for catastrophic consequences if they strike Earth, with a notable example being the [Chicxulub impact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicxulub_impact), widely thought to have induced the [Cretaceous–Paleogene mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous–Paleogene_extinction_event).

In 2006, the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union) (IAU) introduced the currently preferred broad term [small Solar System body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Solar_System_bodies), defined as an object in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System) that is neither a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet), a [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet), nor a [natural satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite); this includes asteroids, comets, [centaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaur_(small_Solar_System_body)), [trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_(celestial_body)), and [trans-Neptunian objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_objects).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-8) According to IAU, "the term 'minor planet' may still be used, but generally, 'Small Solar System Body' will be preferred."[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-9)

Historically, the first discovered asteroid, [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)), was at first considered a new planet.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-10) It was followed by the discovery of other similar bodies, which with the equipment of the time appeared to be points of light like stars, showing little or no planetary disc, though readily distinguishable from stars due to their apparent motions. This prompted the astronomer Sir [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel) to propose the term asteroid,[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-14) coined in Greek as ἀστεροειδής, or asteroeidēs, meaning 'star-like, star-shaped', and derived from the Ancient Greek [ἀστήρ](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ἀστήρ) astēr 'star, planet'. In the early second half of the 19th century, the terms asteroid and planet (not always qualified as "minor") were still used interchangeably.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-16)

Traditionally, small bodies orbiting the Sun were classified as [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet), asteroids, or [meteoroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoroid), with anything smaller than one meter across being called a meteoroid. The term asteroid, never officially defined,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-17) can be informally used to mean "an irregularly shaped rocky body orbiting the Sun that does not qualify as a planet or a dwarf planet under the IAU definitions".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-18) The main difference between an asteroid and a comet is that a comet shows a coma (tail) due to [sublimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outgassing) of its near-surface ices by solar radiation. A few objects were first classified as minor planets but later showed evidence of cometary activity. Conversely, some (perhaps all) comets are eventually depleted of their surface [volatile ices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_(astrogeology)) and become asteroid-like. A further distinction is that comets typically have more eccentric orbits than most asteroids; highly eccentric asteroids are probably dormant or extinct comets.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-19)

The minor planets beyond Jupiter's orbit are sometimes also called "asteroids", especially in popular presentations.[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-21) However, it is becoming increasingly common for the term asteroid to be restricted to minor planets of the inner Solar System.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-KBOasteroids-22) Therefore, this article will restrict itself for the most part to the classical asteroids: objects of the [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt), [Jupiter trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan), and [near-Earth objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_object).

For almost two centuries after the discovery of [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)) in 1801, all known asteroids spent most of their time at or within the orbit of Jupiter, though a few, such as [944 Hidalgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/944_Hidalgo), ventured farther for part of their orbit. Starting in 1977 with [2060 Chiron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2060_Chiron), astronomers discovered small bodies that permanently resided further out than Jupiter, now called [centaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet)). In 1992, [15760 Albion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/15760_Albion) was discovered, the first object beyond the orbit of [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune) (other than [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto)); soon large numbers of similar objects were observed, now called [trans-Neptunian objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object). Further out are [Kuiper-belt objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt), [scattered-disc objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattered-disc_object), and the much more distant [Oort cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud), hypothesized to be the main reservoir of dormant comets. They inhabit the cold outer reaches of the Solar System where ices remain solid and comet-like bodies exhibit little cometary activity; if centaurs or trans-Neptunian objects were to venture close to the Sun, their volatile ices would [sublimate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)), and traditional approaches would classify them as comets.

The [Kuiper-belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt) bodies are called "objects" partly to avoid the need to classify them as asteroids or comets.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-KBOasteroids-22) They are thought to be predominantly comet-like in composition, though some may be more akin to asteroids.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-23) Most do not have the highly eccentric orbits associated with comets, and the ones so far discovered are larger than traditional [comet nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_nucleus). Other recent observations, such as the analysis of the cometary dust collected by the [Stardust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)) probe, are increasingly blurring the distinction between comets and asteroids,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:0-2) suggesting "a continuum between asteroids and comets" rather than a sharp dividing line.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-:1-3)

In 2006, the IAU created the class of [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet) for the largest minor planets—those massive enough to have become ellipsoidal under their own gravity. Only the largest object in the asteroid belt has been placed in this category: [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)), at about 975 km (606 mi) across.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dwarf1-24)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dwarf2-25) Asteroids have been distinguished from the concept of [planetoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetoid) by describing them respectively as [undifferentiated and differentiated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_differentiation), like dwarf planets.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-f957-26)

History of observations

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=2)]

Despite their large numbers, asteroids are a relatively recent discovery, with the first one—Ceres—only being identified in 1801.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-cunningham2001-27) Only one asteroid, [4 Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta), which has a relatively [reflective surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albedo), is normally visible to the naked eye in dark skies when it is favorably positioned. Rarely, small asteroids passing close to Earth may be briefly visible to the naked eye.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-SPACE-2004-02-04-28) As of May 2025[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit), the [Minor Planet Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center) had data on 1,460,356 minor planets in the inner and outer Solar System, of which about 826,864 had enough information to be given numbered designations.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-MPCcount-29)

Discovery of Ceres

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=3)]



[Giuseppe Velasquez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Velasquez), Giuseppe Piazzi and Ceres, oil on canvas, c. 1803

In 1772, German astronomer [Johann Elert Bode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Elert_Bode), citing [Johann Daniel Titius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Daniel_Titius), published a numerical procession known as the [Titius–Bode law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titius–Bode_law) (now discredited). Except for an unexplained gap between Mars and Jupiter, Bode's formula seemed to predict the orbits of the known planets.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hoskin-30)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hogg1948-31) He wrote the following explanation for the existence of a "missing planet":

This latter point seems in particular to follow from the astonishing relation which the known six planets observe in their distances from the Sun. Let the distance from the Sun to Saturn be taken as 100, then Mercury is separated by 4 such parts from the Sun. Venus is 4 + 3 = 7. The Earth 4 + 6 = 10. Mars 4 + 12 = 16. Now comes a gap in this so orderly progression. After Mars there follows a space of 4 + 24 = 28 parts, in which no planet has yet been seen. Can one believe that the Founder of the universe had left this space empty? Certainly not. From here we come to the distance of Jupiter by 4 + 48 = 52 parts, and finally to that of Saturn by 4 + 96 = 100 parts.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-discovery-32)

Bode's formula predicted another planet would be found with an orbital radius near 2.8 [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit) (AU), or 420 million km, from the Sun.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hogg1948-31) The Titius–Bode law got a boost with [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel)'s discovery of [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus) near the predicted distance for a planet beyond [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn).[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hoskin-30) In 1800, a group headed by [Franz Xaver von Zach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Xaver_von_Zach), editor of the German astronomical journal Monatliche Correspondenz (Monthly Correspondence), sent requests to 24 experienced astronomers (whom he dubbed the "[celestial police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_police)"),[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hogg1948-31) asking that they combine their efforts and begin a methodical search for the expected planet.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hogg1948-31) Although they did not discover Ceres, they later found the asteroids [2 Pallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Pallas), [3 Juno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_Juno) and [4 Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hogg1948-31)

One of the astronomers selected for the search was [Giuseppe Piazzi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Piazzi), a Catholic [priest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Priest) at the Academy of Palermo, Sicily. Before receiving his invitation to join the group, Piazzi discovered Ceres on 1 January 1801.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NASA-20160126-33) He was searching for "the 87th [star] of the Catalogue of the Zodiacal stars of [Mr la Caille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Louis_de_Lacaille)",[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hoskin-30) but found that "it was preceded by another".[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hoskin-30) Instead of a star, Piazzi had found a moving star-like object, which he first thought was a comet:[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Forbes1971-34)

The light was a little faint, and of the colour of[Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter), but similar to many others which generally are reckoned of the eighth[magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude). Therefore I had no doubt of its being any other than a fixed star. [...] The evening of the third, my suspicion was converted into certainty, being assured it was not a fixed star. Nevertheless before I made it known, I waited till the evening of the fourth, when I had the satisfaction to see it had moved at the same rate as on the preceding days.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hoskin-30)

Piazzi observed Ceres a total of 24 times, the final time on 11 February 1801, when illness interrupted his work. He announced his discovery on 24 January 1801 in letters to only two fellow astronomers, his compatriot [Barnaba Oriani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barnaba_Oriani) of Milan and Bode in Berlin.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-cunningham2001-27) He reported it as a comet but "since its movement is so slow and rather uniform, it has occurred to me several times that it might be something better than a comet".[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hoskin-30) In April, Piazzi sent his complete observations to Oriani, Bode, and French astronomer [Jérôme Lalande](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jérôme_Lalande). The information was published in the September 1801 issue of the Monatliche Correspondenz.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Forbes1971-34)

By this time, the apparent position of Ceres had changed (mostly due to Earth's motion around the Sun), and was too close to the Sun's glare for other astronomers to confirm Piazzi's observations. Toward the end of the year, Ceres should have been visible again, but after such a long time it was difficult to predict its exact position. To recover Ceres, mathematician [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss), then 24 years old, developed an [efficient method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss's_method) of [orbit determination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit_determination).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Forbes1971-34) In a few weeks, he predicted the path of Ceres and sent his results to von Zach. On 31 December 1801, von Zach and fellow celestial policeman [Heinrich W. M. Olbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Wilhelm_Matthäus_Olbers) found Ceres near the predicted position and thus recovered it.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Forbes1971-34) At 2.8 AU from the Sun, Ceres appeared to fit the Titius–Bode law almost perfectly; however, Neptune, once discovered in 1846, was 8 AU closer than predicted, leading most astronomers to conclude that the law was a coincidence.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-35) Piazzi named the newly discovered object Ceres Ferdinandea, "in honor of the [patron goddess of Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(Roman_mythology)) and of [King Ferdinand of Bourbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_the_Two_Sicilies)".[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-discovery-32)

Further search

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=4)]



Three other asteroids ([2 Pallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Pallas), [3 Juno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_Juno), and [4 Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta)) were discovered by von Zach's group over the next few years, with Vesta found in 1807.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hogg1948-31) No new asteroids were discovered until 1845. Amateur astronomer [Karl Ludwig Hencke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Ludwig_Hencke) started his searches of new asteroids in 1830, and fifteen years later, while looking for Vesta, he found the asteroid later named [5 Astraea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5_Astraea). It was the first new asteroid discovery in 38 years. [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss) was given the honor of naming the asteroid. After this, other astronomers joined; 15 asteroids were found by the end of 1851. In 1868, when [James Craig Watson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Craig_Watson) discovered the 100th asteroid, the [French Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences) engraved the faces of [Karl Theodor Robert Luther](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Theodor_Robert_Luther), [John Russell Hind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Russell_Hind), and [Hermann Goldschmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Mayer_Salomon_Goldschmidt), the three most successful asteroid-hunters at that time, on a commemorative medallion marking the event.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dawn-community-36)

In 1891, [Max Wolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximilian_Franz_Joseph_Cornelius_Wolf) pioneered the use of [astrophotography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophotography) to detect asteroids, which appeared as short streaks on long-exposure photographic plates.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dawn-community-36) This dramatically increased the rate of detection compared with earlier visual methods: Wolf alone discovered 248 asteroids, beginning with [323 Brucia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/323_Brucia),[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-37) whereas only slightly more than 300 had been discovered up to that point. It was known that there were many more, but most astronomers did not bother with them, some calling them "vermin of the skies",[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-38) a phrase variously attributed to [Eduard Suess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard_Suess)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-39) and [Edmund Weiss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Weiss).[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-40) Even a century later, only a few thousand asteroids were identified, numbered and named.

19th and 20th centuries

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=5)]



In the past, asteroids were discovered by a four-step process. First, a region of the sky was [photographed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophotography) by a wide-field [telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope) or [astrograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrograph). Pairs of photographs were taken, typically one hour apart. Multiple pairs could be taken over a series of days. Second, the two films or [plates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photographic_plate) of the same region were viewed under a [stereoscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereoscope). A body in orbit around the Sun would move slightly between the pair of films. Under the stereoscope, the image of the body would seem to float slightly above the background of stars. Third, once a moving body was identified, its location would be measured precisely using a digitizing microscope. The location would be measured relative to known star locations.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-41)

These first three steps do not constitute asteroid discovery: the observer has only found an apparition, which gets a [provisional designation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy), made up of the year of discovery, a letter representing the half-month of discovery, and finally a letter and a number indicating the discovery's sequential number (example: 1998 FJ74). The last step is sending the locations and time of observations to the [Minor Planet Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center), where computer programs determine whether an apparition ties together earlier apparitions into a single orbit. If so, the object receives a catalogue number and the observer of the first apparition with a calculated orbit is declared the discoverer, and granted the honor of naming the object subject to the approval of the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-42)

Naming

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=6)]



[2013 EC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2013_EC), shown here in radar images, has a provisional designation

By 1851, the [Royal Astronomical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society) decided that asteroids were being discovered at such a rapid rate that a different system was needed to categorize or name asteroids. In 1852, when [de Gasparis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annibale_de_Gasparis) discovered the twentieth asteroid, [Benjamin Valz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Valz) gave it a name and a number designating its rank among asteroid discoveries, [20 Massalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/20_Massalia). Sometimes asteroids were discovered and not seen again. So, starting in 1892, new asteroids were listed by the year and a capital letter indicating the order in which the asteroid's orbit was calculated and registered within that specific year. For example, the first two asteroids discovered in 1892 were labeled 1892A and 1892B. However, there were not enough letters in the alphabet for all of the asteroids discovered in 1893, so 1893Z was followed by 1893AA. A number of variations of these methods were tried, including designations that included year plus a Greek letter in 1914. A simple chronological numbering system was established in 1925.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dawn-community-36)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-43)

Currently all newly discovered asteroids receive a [provisional designation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy) (such as [2002 AT4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_AT4)) consisting of the year of discovery and an alphanumeric code indicating the [half-month](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-month) of discovery and the sequence within that half-month. Once an asteroid's orbit has been confirmed, it is given a number, and later may also be given a name (e.g. [433 Eros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/433_Eros)). The formal naming convention uses parentheses around the number—e.g. (433) Eros—but dropping the parentheses is quite common. Informally, it is also common to drop the number altogether, or to drop it after the first mention when a name is repeated in running text.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-OpenUNamingAstrds-44) In addition, names can be proposed by the asteroid's discoverer, within guidelines established by the International Astronomical Union.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-PlanSocNameGuides-45)

Symbols

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=7)]

The first asteroids to be discovered were assigned iconic symbols like the ones traditionally used to designate the planets. By 1852 there were two dozen asteroid symbols, which often occurred in multiple variants.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Gould-1852-46)

In 1851, after the fifteenth asteroid, [Eunomia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/15_Eunomia), had been discovered, [Johann Franz Encke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Franz_Encke) made a major change in the upcoming 1854 edition of the [Berliner Astronomisches Jahrbuch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berliner_Astronomisches_Jahrbuch) (BAJ, Berlin Astronomical Yearbook). He introduced a disk (circle), a traditional symbol for a star, as the generic symbol for an asteroid. The circle was then numbered in order of discovery to indicate a specific asteroid. The numbered-circle convention was quickly adopted by astronomers, and the next asteroid to be discovered ([16 Psyche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16_Psyche), in 1852) was the first to be designated in that way at the time of its discovery. However, Psyche was given an iconic symbol as well, as were a few other asteroids discovered over the next few years. [20 Massalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/20_Massalia) was the first asteroid that was not assigned an iconic symbol, and no iconic symbols were created after the 1855 discovery of [37 Fides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/37_Fides).[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-47)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hilton-2011-a-48)

Formation

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=8)]

Many asteroids are the shattered remnants of [planetesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetesimal), bodies within the young Sun's [solar nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_nebula) that never grew large enough to become [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet).[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-CNEOS-FAQ-49) It is thought that planetesimals in the asteroid belt evolved much like the rest of objects in the solar nebula until Jupiter neared its current mass, at which point excitation from [orbital resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance) with Jupiter ejected over 99% of planetesimals in the belt. Simulations and a discontinuity in spin rate and spectral properties suggest that asteroids larger than approximately 120 km (75 mi) in diameter [accreted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)) during that early era, whereas smaller bodies are fragments from collisions between asteroids during or after the Jovian disruption.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-50) Ceres and Vesta grew large enough to melt and [differentiate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_differentiation), with heavy metallic elements sinking to the core, leaving rocky minerals in the crust.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-ACM-51)

In the [Nice model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nice_model), many [Kuiper-belt objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt) are captured in the outer asteroid belt, at distances greater than 2.6 AU. Most were later ejected by Jupiter, but those that remained may be the [D-type asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-type_asteroid), and possibly include Ceres.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-52)

Distribution within the Solar System

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=9)]





Various dynamical groups of asteroids have been discovered orbiting in the inner Solar System. Their orbits are perturbed by the gravity of other bodies in the Solar System and by the [Yarkovsky effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yarkovsky_effect). Significant populations include:

Asteroid belt

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=10)]

The majority of known asteroids orbit within the asteroid belt between the orbits of [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars) and [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter), generally in relatively low-[eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity) (i.e. not very elongated) orbits. This belt is estimated to contain between 1.1 and 1.9 million asteroids larger than 1 km (0.6 mi) in diameter,[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-53) and millions of smaller ones. These asteroids may be remnants of the [protoplanetary disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk), and in this region the [accretion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)) of [planetesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetesimal) into planets during the formative period of the Solar System was prevented by large gravitational perturbations by [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter).

Contrary to popular imagery, the asteroid belt is mostly empty. The asteroids are spread over such a large volume that reaching an asteroid without aiming carefully would be improbable. Nonetheless, hundreds of thousands of asteroids are currently known, and the total number ranges in the millions or more, depending on the lower size cutoff. Over 200 asteroids are known to be larger than 100 km,[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-54) and a survey in the infrared wavelengths has shown that the asteroid belt has between 700,000 and 1.7 million asteroids with a diameter of 1 km or more.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-55) The [absolute magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude) of most of the known asteroids are between 11 and 19, with the median at about 16.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-mpc-56)

The total mass of the asteroid belt is estimated to be 2.39×1021 kg, which is just 3% of the mass of the Moon; the mass of the [Kuiper Belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt) and [Scattered Disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattered_disc) is over 100 times as large.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pitjeva2018-57) The four largest objects, Ceres, Vesta, Pallas, and [Hygiea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10_Hygiea), account for maybe 62% of the belt's total mass, with 39% accounted for by Ceres alone.

Trojans

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=11)]

Trojans are populations that share an orbit with a larger planet or moon, but do not collide with it because they orbit in one of the two [Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point) of stability, L4 and L5, which lie 60° ahead of and behind the larger body.

In the Solar System, most known trojans share the [orbit of Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan). They are divided into the [Greek camp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_camp) at L4 (ahead of Jupiter) and the [Trojan camp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_camp) at L5 (trailing Jupiter). More than a million Jupiter trojans larger than one kilometer are thought to exist,[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Yoshida2006-58) of which more than 7,000 are currently catalogued. In other planetary orbits only nine [Mars trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_trojan), 28 [Neptune trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune_trojan), two [Uranus trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus_trojan), and two [Earth trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_trojan), have been found to date. A temporary [Venus trojan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2013_ND15) is also known. Numerical orbital dynamics stability simulations indicate that Saturn and Uranus probably do not have any primordial trojans.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-sheppard2006-59)

Near-Earth asteroids

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=12)]

[Near-Earth asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroids), or NEAs, are asteroids that have orbits that pass close to that of Earth. Asteroids that actually cross Earth's orbital path are known as Earth-crossers. As of April 2022[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit), a total of 28,772 near-Earth asteroids were known; 878 have a diameter of one kilometer or larger.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nasa_neo-60)

A small number of NEAs are [extinct comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinct_comets) that have lost their volatile surface materials, although having a faint or intermittent comet-like tail does not necessarily result in a classification as a near-Earth comet, making the boundaries somewhat fuzzy. The rest of the near-Earth asteroids are driven out of the asteroid belt by gravitational interactions with [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter).[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-MorbidelliAstIII-61)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-62)

Many asteroids have [natural satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite) ([minor-planet moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor-planet_moon)). As of October 2021[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit), there were 85 NEAs known to have at least one moon, including three known to have two moons.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-63) The asteroid [3122 Florence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3122_Florence), one of the largest potentially hazardous asteroids with a diameter of 4.5 km (2.8 mi), has two moons measuring 100–300 m (330–980 ft) across, which were discovered by radar imaging during the asteroid's 2017 approach to Earth.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Florence-moons-64)

Near-Earth asteroids are divided into groups based on their [semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis) (a), [perihelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis) distance (q), and [aphelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis) distance (Q):[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NEO-groups-65)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-MorbidelliAstIII-61)

The [Atiras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atira_asteroid)or Apoheles have orbits strictly inside Earth's orbit: an Atira asteroid's aphelion distance (Q) is smaller than Earth's perihelion distance (0.983 AU). That is, Q < 0.983 AU, which implies that the asteroid's semi-major axis is also less than 0.983 AU.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-atiras-66)The [Atens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aten_asteroid)have a semi-major axis of less than 1 AU and cross Earth's orbit. Mathematically, a < 1.0 AU and Q > 0.983 AU. (0.983 AU is Earth's perihelion distance.)The [Apollos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_asteroid)have a semi-major axis of more than 1 AU and cross Earth's orbit. Mathematically, a > 1.0 AU and q < 1.017 AU. (1.017 AU is Earth's aphelion distance.)The [Amors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amor_asteroid)have orbits strictly outside Earth's orbit: an Amor asteroid's perihelion distance (q) is greater than Earth's aphelion distance (1.017 AU). Amor asteroids are also near-earth objects so q < 1.3 AU. In summary, 1.017 AU < q < 1.3 AU. (This implies that the asteroid's semi-major axis (a) is also larger than 1.017 AU.) Some Amor asteroid orbits cross the orbit of Mars.

Martian moons

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=13)]

It is unclear whether Martian moons [Phobos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos_(moon)) and [Deimos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deimos_(moon)) are captured asteroids or were formed due to impact event on Mars.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-burns-67): 1284–1285 Phobos and Deimos both have much in common with carbonaceous [C-type asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-type_asteroid), with [spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum), [albedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albedo), and [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density) very similar to those of C- or D-type asteroids.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-c-type-68) Based on their similarity, one hypothesis is that both moons may be captured [main-belt asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-belt_asteroids).[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-69)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-landis-70) Both moons have very circular orbits which lie almost exactly in Mars's [equatorial plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_plane), and hence a capture origin requires a mechanism for circularizing the initially highly eccentric orbit, and adjusting its inclination into the equatorial plane, most probably by a combination of atmospheric drag and [tidal forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_force),[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-cazenave-71) although it is not clear whether sufficient time was available for this to occur for Deimos.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-burns-67): 1300–1301 Capture also requires dissipation of energy. The current Martian atmosphere is too thin to capture a Phobos-sized object by atmospheric braking.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-burns-67)[[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources)] [Geoffrey A. Landis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_A._Landis) has pointed out that the capture could have occurred if the original body was a [binary asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_asteroid) that separated under tidal forces.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-landis-70)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-72)

Phobos could be a second-generation Solar System object that [coalesced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)) in orbit after Mars formed, rather than forming concurrently out of the same birth cloud as Mars.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-ESA2010-73)

Another hypothesis is that Mars was once surrounded by many Phobos- and Deimos-sized bodies, perhaps ejected into orbit around it by a collision with a large [planetesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetesimal).[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Craddock-74) The high porosity of the interior of Phobos (based on the density of 1.88 g/cm3, voids are estimated to comprise 25 to 35 percent of Phobos's volume) is inconsistent with an asteroidal origin.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Andert-75) Observations of Phobos in the [thermal infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_infrared) suggest a composition containing mainly [phyllosilicates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phyllosilicates), which are well known from the surface of Mars. The spectra are distinct from those of all classes of [chondrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chondrite) meteorites, again pointing away from an asteroidal origin.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Giuranna-76) Both sets of findings support an origin of Phobos from material ejected by an impact on Mars that reaccreted in Martian orbit,[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Blast-77) similar to the [prevailing theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis) for the origin of Earth's moon.

Characteristics

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=14)]

Size distribution

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=15)]



[1 Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_Ceres)(blue),

[4 Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta),

[2 Pallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Pallas),

[10 Hygiea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10_Hygiea),

[704 Interamnia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/704_Interamnia),

[15 Eunomia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/15_Eunomia)and the remainder of the Main Belt (pink). The unit of mass is ×1018 kg.

Asteroids vary greatly in size, from almost 1,000 km (620 mi) for the largest down to rocks just 1 m (3.3 ft) across, below which an object is classified as a [meteoroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoroid).[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-78) The three largest are very much like miniature planets: they are roughly spherical, have at least partly differentiated interiors,[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Schmidt2007-79) and are thought to be surviving [protoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanet). The vast majority, however, are much smaller and are irregularly shaped; they are thought to be either battered [planetesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetesimal) or fragments of larger bodies.

The [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet)[Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)) is by far the largest asteroid, with a diameter of 940 km (580 mi). The next largest are [4 Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta) and [2 Pallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Pallas), both with diameters of just over 500 km (300 mi). Vesta is the brightest of the four main-belt asteroids that can, on occasion, be visible to the naked eye.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-80) On some rare occasions, a near-Earth asteroid may briefly become visible without technical aid; see [99942 Apophis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/99942_Apophis).

The mass of all the objects of the [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt), lying between the orbits of [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars) and [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter), is estimated to be (2394±6)×1018 kg, ≈3.25% of the mass of the Moon. Of this, [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)) comprises 938×1018 kg, about 40% of the total. Adding in the next three most massive objects, [Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta) (11%), [Pallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Pallas) (8.5%), and [Hygiea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10_Hygiea) (3–4%), brings this figure up to a bit over 60%, whereas the next seven most-massive asteroids bring the total up to 70%.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Pitjeva2018-57) The number of asteroids increases rapidly as their individual masses decrease.

The number of asteroids decreases markedly with increasing size. Although the size distribution generally follows a [power law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_law), there are 'bumps' at about 5 km and 100 km, where more asteroids than expected from such a curve are found. Most asteroids larger than approximately 120 kilometres (75 mi) in diameter are primordial (surviving from the accretion epoch), whereas most smaller asteroids are products of fragmentation of primordial asteroids. The primordial population of the main belt was probably 200 times what it is today.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-81)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-82)

Largest asteroids

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=16)]

[ESO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory)'s

[Very Large Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope)

Three largest objects in the asteroid belt, [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)), [Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta), and [Pallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Pallas), are intact [protoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanet) that share many characteristics common to planets, and are atypical compared to the majority of irregularly shaped asteroids. The fourth-largest asteroid, [Hygiea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10_Hygiea), appears nearly spherical although it may have an undifferentiated interior,[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-83) like the majority of asteroids. The four largest asteroids constitute about five-eighths of the mass of the asteroid belt; Ceres and Vesta alone constitute half.

Ceres is the only asteroid that appears to have a [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)) shape under its own gravity and hence the only one that is a [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet).[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-IAU-2006-84) It has a much higher [absolute magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude#Solar_System_bodies_(H)) than the other asteroids, of around 3.32,[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-AstJ-2002-v123-p549-85) and may possess a surface layer of ice.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-planetary-86) Like the planets, Ceres is differentiated: it has a crust, a mantle and a core.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-planetary-86) No meteorites from Ceres have been found on Earth.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-satellites-87)

Vesta, too, has a differentiated interior, though it formed inside the Solar System's [frost line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)), and so is devoid of water;[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-88)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-89) its composition is mainly of [basaltic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basaltic_rock) rock with minerals such as [olivine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olivine).[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-90) Aside from the large crater at its southern pole, [Rheasilvia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheasilvia), Vesta also has an ellipsoidal shape. Vesta is the parent body of the [Vestian family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vestian_family) and other [V-type asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-type_asteroid), and is the source of the [HED meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HED_meteorite), which constitute 5% of all meteorites on Earth.

Pallas is unusual in that, like [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus), it rotates on its side, with its axis of rotation tilted at high angles to its orbital plane.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Torppa1996-91) Its composition is similar to that of Ceres: high in [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon) and [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon), and perhaps partially differentiated.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Icarus-1983-v56-p398-92) Pallas is the parent body of the [Palladian family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladian_family) of asteroids.

Hygiea is the largest [carbonaceous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonaceous_asteroid) asteroid[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Icarus-2002-156-p202-93) and, unlike the other largest asteroids, lies relatively close to the [plane of the ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic). It is the largest member and presumed parent body of the [Hygiean family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygiean_family) of asteroids. Because there is no sufficiently large crater on the surface to be the source of that family, as there is on Vesta, it is thought that Hygiea may have been completely disrupted in the collision that formed the Hygiean family and recoalesced after losing a bit less than 2% of its mass. Observations taken with the [Very Large Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope)'s [SPHERE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VLT-SPHERE) imager in 2017 and 2018, revealed that Hygiea has a nearly spherical shape, which is consistent both with it being in [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium), or formerly being in hydrostatic equilibrium, or with being disrupted and recoalescing.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NatAstr-2019-10-28-94)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Strickland2019-95)

Internal differentiation of large asteroids is possibly related to their lack of [natural satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite), as satellites of main belt asteroids are mostly believed to form from collisional disruption, creating a [rubble pile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubble_pile) structure.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-satellites-87)

Rotation

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=17)]

Measurements of the rotation rates of large asteroids in the asteroid belt show that there is an upper limit. Very few asteroids with a diameter larger than 100 meters have a rotation period less than 2.2 hours.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-96) For asteroids rotating faster than approximately this rate, the inertial force at the surface is greater than the gravitational force, so any loose surface material would be flung out. However, a solid object should be able to rotate much more rapidly. This suggests that most asteroids with a diameter over 100 meters are [rubble piles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubble_pile) formed through the accumulation of debris after collisions between asteroids.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Rossi-2004-97)

Color

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=18)]

Asteroids become darker and redder with age due to [space weathering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weathering).[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-UHi2005-05-19-98) However evidence suggests most of the color change occurs rapidly, in the first hundred thousand years, limiting the usefulness of spectral measurement for determining the age of asteroids.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Courtland-2009-99)

Surface features

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=19)]



Except for the "[big four](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_exceptional_asteroids#Largest_by_mass)" (Ceres, Pallas, Vesta, and Hygiea), asteroids are likely to be broadly similar in appearance, if irregular in shape. 50 km (31 mi) [253 Mathilde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/253_Mathilde) is a rubble pile saturated with craters with diameters the size of the asteroid's radius. Earth-based observations of 300 km (190 mi) [511 Davida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/511_Davida), one of the largest asteroids after the big four, reveal a similarly angular profile, suggesting it is also saturated with radius-size craters.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Icarus-2007-v191-p616-100) Medium-sized asteroids such as Mathilde and [243 Ida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/243_Ida), that have been observed up close, also reveal a deep [regolith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regolith) covering the surface. Of the big four, Pallas and Hygiea are practically unknown. Vesta has compression fractures encircling a radius-size crater at its south pole but is otherwise a [spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroid).

[Dawn spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawn_(spacecraft)) revealed that Ceres has a heavily cratered surface, but with fewer large craters than expected.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-marchi-101) Models based on the formation of the current asteroid belt had suggested Ceres should possess 10 to 15 craters larger than 400 km (250 mi) in diameter.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-marchi-101) The largest confirmed crater on Ceres, [Kerwan Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerwan_(crater)), is 284 km (176 mi) across.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-102) The most likely reason for this is [viscous relaxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscoelasticity) of the crust slowly flattening out larger impacts.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-marchi-101)

Composition

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=20)]

Asteroids are classified by their characteristic [emission spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum), with the majority falling into three main groups: [C-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-type_asteroid), [M-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-type_asteroid), and [S-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-type_asteroid). These describe carbonaceous ([carbon-rich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon)), [metallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal), and [silicaceous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silica) (stony) compositions, respectively. The physical composition of asteroids is varied and in most cases poorly understood. Ceres appears to be composed of a rocky core covered by an icy mantle; Vesta is thought to have a [nickel-iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel-iron) core, [olivine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olivine) mantle, and basaltic crust.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Hubble-Vespa-1995-04-19-103) Thought to be the largest undifferentiated asteroid, [10 Hygiea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10_Hygiea) seems to have a uniformly primitive composition of [carbonaceous chondrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite), but it may actually be a differentiated asteroid that was globally disrupted by an impact and then reassembled. Other asteroids appear to be the remnant cores or mantles of proto-planets, high in rock and metal. Most small asteroids are believed to be piles of rubble held together loosely by gravity, although the largest are probably solid. Some asteroids have [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_moon) or are co-orbiting [binaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_asteroid): rubble piles, moons, binaries, and scattered [asteroid families](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_family) are thought to be the results of collisions that disrupted a parent asteroid, or possibly a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disrupted_planet).[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-ARX-20060816-104)

In the main asteroid belt, there appear to be two primary populations of asteroid: a dark, volatile-rich population, consisting of the [C-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-type_asteroid) and [P-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-type_asteroid) asteroids, with albedos less than 0.10 and densities under 2.2 g/cm3, and a dense, volatile-poor population, consisting of the [S-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-type_asteroid) and [M-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-type_asteroid) asteroids, with albedos over 0.15 and densities greater than 2.7. Within these populations, larger asteroids are denser, presumably due to compression. There appears to be minimal macro-porosity (interstitial vacuum) in the score of asteroids with masses greater than 10×1018 kg.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-VLT-105)

Composition is calculated from three primary sources: [albedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albedo), surface spectrum, and density. The last can only be determined accurately by observing the orbits of moons the asteroid might have. So far, every asteroid with moons has turned out to be a rubble pile, a loose conglomeration of rock and metal that may be half empty space by volume. The investigated asteroids are as large as 280 km in diameter, and include [121 Hermione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/121_Hermione) (268×186×183 km), and [87 Sylvia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/87_Sylvia) (384×262×232 km). Few asteroids are [larger than 87 Sylvia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_notable_asteroids#Largest_by_diameter), none of them have moons. The fact that such large asteroids as Sylvia may be rubble piles, presumably due to disruptive impacts, has important consequences for the formation of the Solar System: computer simulations of collisions involving solid bodies show them destroying each other as often as merging, but colliding rubble piles are more likely to merge. This means that the cores of the planets could have formed relatively quickly.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Icarus-2011-02-p1022-106)

Water

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=21)]

Scientists hypothesize that some of the first water brought to Earth was delivered by asteroid impacts after the collision that produced the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon).[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Campins2010-107) In 2009, the presence of [water ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice) was confirmed on the surface of [24 Themis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24_Themis) using NASA's [Infrared Telescope Facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_Telescope_Facility). The surface of the asteroid appears completely covered in ice. As this ice layer is [sublimating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)), it may be getting replenished by a reservoir of ice under the surface. Organic compounds were also detected on the surface.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Cowen-2009-108)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Atkinson-2009-109)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Campins2010-107)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-RivkinEmery2010-110) The presence of ice on 24 Themis makes the initial theory plausible.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Campins2010-107)

In October 2013, water was detected on an extrasolar body for the first time, on an asteroid orbiting the [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf)[GD 61](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GD_61).[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-111) On 22 January 2014, [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency) (ESA) scientists reported the detection, for the first definitive time, of [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor) on [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)), the largest object in the asteroid belt.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-KüppersO'Rourke2014-112) The detection was made by using the [far-infrared abilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-infrared_astronomy) of the [Herschel Space Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory).[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NASA-20140122-113) The finding is unexpected because comets, not asteroids, are typically considered to "sprout jets and plumes". According to one of the scientists, "The lines are becoming more and more blurred between comets and asteroids."[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NASA-20140122-113)

Findings have shown that [solar winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind) can react with the oxygen in the upper layer of the asteroids and create water. It has been estimated that "every cubic metre of irradiated rock could contain up to 20 litres"; study was conducted using an atom probe tomography, numbers are given for the Itokawa S-type asteroid.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-114)[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-115)

Acfer 049, a meteorite discovered in Algeria in 1990, was shown in 2019 to have an ultraporous lithology (UPL): porous texture that could be formed by removal of ice that filled these pores, this suggests that UPL "represent fossils of primordial ice".[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-116)

Organic compounds

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=22)]

Asteroids contain traces of [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid) and other organic compounds, and some speculate that asteroid impacts may have seeded the early Earth with the chemicals necessary to initiate life, or may have even brought life itself to Earth (an event called "[panspermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia)").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-SPACE-2001-12-19-117)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Reuell-2019-118) In August 2011, a report, based on [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA) studies with [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite) found on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth), was published suggesting [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) components ([adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine), [guanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanine) and related [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_molecules)) may have been formed on asteroids and [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet) in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space).[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Callahan-119)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Steigerwald-120)[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-DNA-121)

In November 2019, scientists reported detecting, for the first time, [sugar molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar), including [ribose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribose), in [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite), suggesting that chemical processes on asteroids can produce some fundamentally essential bio-ingredients important to [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life), and supporting the notion of an [RNA world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_world) prior to a DNA-based [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis) on Earth, and possibly, as well, the notion of [panspermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia).[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NASA-20191118-122)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-PNAS-20191118-123)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-124)

Classification

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=23)]

Asteroids are commonly categorized according to two criteria: the characteristics of their orbits, and features of their reflectance [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum).

Orbital classification

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=24)]



[(419624) 2010 SO16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(419624)_2010_SO16)

Many asteroids have been placed in groups and families based on their orbital characteristics. Apart from the broadest divisions, it is customary to name a group of asteroids after the first member of that group to be discovered. Groups are relatively loose dynamical associations, whereas families are tighter and result from the catastrophic break-up of a large parent asteroid sometime in the past.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-AstFams-Icarus-1995-125) Families are more common and easier to identify within the main asteroid belt, but several small families have been reported among the [Jupiter trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan).[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-JewittEtal2004-126) Main belt families were first recognized by [Kiyotsugu Hirayama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiyotsugu_Hirayama) in 1918 and are often called [Hirayama families](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hirayama_families) in his honor.

About 30–35% of the bodies in the asteroid belt belong to dynamical families, each thought to have a common origin in a past collision between asteroids. A family has also been associated with the plutoid [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet)[Haumea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haumea).

Some asteroids have unusual [horseshoe orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit) that are co-orbital with [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) or another planet. Examples are [3753 Cruithne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3753_Cruithne) and [2002 AA29](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_AA29). The first instance of this type of orbital arrangement was discovered between [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn)'s moons [Epimetheus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epimetheus_(moon)) and [Janus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janus_(moon)). Sometimes these horseshoe objects temporarily become [quasi-satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite) for a few decades or a few hundred years, before returning to their earlier status. Both Earth and [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus) are known to have quasi-satellites.

Such objects, if associated with Earth or Venus or even hypothetically [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet)), are a special class of [Aten asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aten_asteroid). However, such objects could be associated with the outer planets as well.

Spectral classification

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=25)]

In 1975, an asteroid [taxonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy_(general)) system based on [color](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color), [albedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albedo), and [spectral shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line) was developed by [Chapman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clark_R._Chapman), [Morrison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Morrison_(astrophysicist)), and [Zellner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Zellner?action=edit&redlink=1).[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-CMZ-1975-Icarus-127) These properties are thought to correspond to the composition of the asteroid's surface material. The original classification system had three categories: [C-types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-type_asteroid) for dark carbonaceous objects (75% of known asteroids), [S-types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-type_asteroid) for stony (silicaceous) objects (17% of known asteroids) and U for those that did not fit into either C or S. This classification has since been expanded to include many other asteroid types. The number of types continues to grow as more asteroids are studied.

The two most widely used taxonomies now used are the [Tholen classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tholen_classification) and [SMASS classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMASS_classification). The former was proposed in 1984 by [David J. Tholen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_J._Tholen), and was based on data collected from an eight-color asteroid survey performed in the 1980s. This resulted in 14 asteroid categories.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Tholen-1989-128) In 2002, the Small Main-Belt Asteroid Spectroscopic Survey resulted in a modified version of the Tholen taxonomy with 24 different types. Both systems have three broad categories of C, S, and X asteroids, where X consists of mostly metallic asteroids, such as the [M-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-type_asteroid). There are also several smaller classes.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Bus-2002-129)

The proportion of known asteroids falling into the various spectral types does not necessarily reflect the proportion of all asteroids that are of that type; some types are easier to detect than others, biasing the totals.

Problems

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=26)]

Originally, spectral designations were based on inferences of an asteroid's composition.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-McSween-1999-130) However, the correspondence between spectral class and composition is not always very good, and a variety of classifications are in use. This has led to significant confusion. Although asteroids of different spectral classifications are likely to be composed of different materials, there are no assurances that asteroids within the same taxonomic class are composed of the same (or similar) materials.

Active asteroids

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=27)]



[(101955) Bennu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/101955_Bennu)seen ejecting particles by the

[OSIRIS-REx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OSIRIS-REx)

Active asteroids are objects that have asteroid-like orbits but show [comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet)-like visual characteristics. That is, they show [comae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma_(cometary)), [tails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_tail), or other visual evidence of mass-loss (like a comet), but their orbit remains within [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter)'s orbit (like an asteroid).[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Jewitt-131)[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-JHA15-132) These bodies were originally designated main-belt comets (MBCs) in 2006 by astronomers [David C. Jewitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_C._Jewitt) and [Henry Hsieh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Hsieh_(astronomer)?action=edit&redlink=1), but this name implies they are necessarily icy in composition like a comet and that they only exist within the [main-belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt), whereas the growing population of active asteroids shows that this is not always the case.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Jewitt-131)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NYT-20190319-133)[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-134)

The first active asteroid discovered is [7968 Elst–Pizarro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7968_Elst–Pizarro). It was discovered (as an asteroid) in 1979 but then was found to have a tail by [Eric Elst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_Walter_Elst) and Guido Pizarro in 1996 and given the cometary designation 133P/Elst-Pizarro.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Jewitt-131)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HH133P-135) Another notable object is [311P/PanSTARRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/311P/PanSTARRS): observations made by the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope) revealed that it had six comet-like tails.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-hubblesite-136) The tails are suspected to be streams of material ejected by the asteroid as a result of a [rubble pile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubble_pile) asteroid spinning fast enough to remove material from it.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Jewitt2013-137)



By smashing into the asteroid [Dimorphos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimorphos), NASA's [Double Asteroid Redirection Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Asteroid_Redirection_Test) spacecraft made it an active asteroid. Scientists had proposed that some active asteroids are the result of impact events, but no one had ever observed the activation of an asteroid. The DART mission activated Dimorphos under precisely known and carefully observed impact conditions, enabling the detailed study of the formation of an active asteroid for the first time.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nasa-march2023-138)[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-139) Observations show that Dimorphos lost approximately 1 million kilograms after the collision.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-140) Impact produced a dust plume that temporarily brightened the Didymos system and developed a 10,000-kilometer (6,200 mi)-long [dust tail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_tail) that persisted for several months.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NOIRLab-20221003-141)[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NASA-20221215-142)[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Li2023-143)

Dark comets

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=28)]

First identified in 2024, dark comets are asteroids that show non-gravitational acceleration, a behavior normally seen in comets but not asteroids, but have no coma or tail, appearing visually as asteroids. Two families have been identified. The Outer Family consists of objects larger than 100 meters, and have orbits that reach out nearly to Jupiter, resembling [Jupiter family comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_family_comets). The Inner Family dark comets were smaller than 50 meters, darker, and had roughly circular orbits.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-dark_comets-144) All dark comets orbit in the main asteroid belt except for [ʻOumuamua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ʻOumuamua), an interstellar object, which was the first asteroid-like object to show non-gravitation acceleration, while appearing visually as an asteroid.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-non-grav-145)

Observation and exploration

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=29)]

Until the age of space travel, objects in the asteroid belt could only be observed with large telescopes, their shapes and terrain remaining a mystery. The best modern ground-based telescopes and the Earth-orbiting [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope) can only resolve a small amount of detail on the surfaces of the largest asteroids. Limited information about the shapes and compositions of asteroids can be inferred from their [light curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_curve) (variation in brightness during rotation) and their spectral properties. Sizes can be estimated by timing the lengths of star occultations (when an asteroid passes directly in front of a star). [Radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar) imaging can yield good information about asteroid shapes and orbital and rotational parameters, especially for near-Earth asteroids. Spacecraft flybys can provide much more data than any ground or space-based observations; sample-return missions gives insights about regolith composition.

Ground-based observations

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=30)]





[(505657) 2014 SR339](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(505657)_2014_SR339)as seen by Arecibo

As asteroids are rather small and faint objects, the data that can be obtained from ground-based observations (GBO) are limited. By means of ground-based optical telescopes the visual magnitude can be obtained; when converted into the absolute magnitude it gives a rough estimate of the asteroid's size. Light-curve measurements can also be made by GBO; when collected over a long period of time it allows an estimate of the rotational period, the pole orientation (sometimes), and a rough estimate of the asteroid's shape. Spectral data (both visible-light and near-infrared spectroscopy) gives information about the object's composition, used to classify the observed asteroids. Such observations are limited as they provide information about only the thin layer on the surface (up to several micrometers).[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-michel-146) As planetologist [Patrick Michel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrick_Michel) writes:

Mid- to thermal-infrared observations, along with polarimetry measurements, are probably the only data that give some indication of actual physical properties. Measuring the heat flux of an asteroid at a single wavelength gives an estimate of the dimensions of the object; these measurements have lower uncertainty than measurements of the reflected sunlight in the visible-light spectral region. If the two measurements can be combined, both the effective diameter and the geometric albedo—the latter being a measure of the brightness at zero phase angle, that is, when illumination comes from directly behind the observer—can be derived. In addition, thermal measurements at two or more wavelengths, plus the brightness in the visible-light region, give information on the thermal properties. The thermal inertia, which is a measure of how fast a material heats up or cools off, of most observed asteroids is lower than the bare-rock reference value but greater than that of the lunar regolith; this observation indicates the presence of an insulating layer of granular material on their surface.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-michel-146)

Near-Earth asteroids that come into close vicinity of the planet can be studied in more details with [radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar); it provides information about the surface of the asteroid (for example can show the presence of craters and boulders). Such observations were conducted by the [Arecibo Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory) in Puerto Rico (305 meter dish) and [Goldstone Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldstone_Observatory) in California (70 meter dish). Radar observations can also be used for accurate determination of the orbital and rotational dynamics of observed objects.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-michel-146)

Space-based observations

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=31)]



[WISE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer)infrared space telescope



[6481 Tenzing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6481_Tenzing), center, is seen moving against a background of stars in this series of images taken by the

[James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope)'s instrument

[NIRCam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIRCam).

Both space and ground-based observatories conducted asteroid search programs; the space-based searches are expected to detect more objects because there is no atmosphere to interfere and because they can observe larger portions of the sky. [NEOWISE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NEOWISE) observed more than 100,000 asteroids of the main belt, [Spitzer Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope) observed more than 700 near-Earth asteroids. These observations determined rough sizes of the majority of observed objects, but provided limited detail about surface properties (such as regolith depth and composition, angle of repose, cohesion, and porosity).[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-michel-146)

Asteroids were also studied by the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope), such as tracking the colliding asteroids in the main belt,[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-147)[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-148) break-up of an asteroid,[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-149) observing an [active asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_asteroid) with six comet-like tails,[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-150) and observing asteroids that were chosen as targets of dedicated missions.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-151)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-152)

Space probe missions

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=32)]

According to [Patrick Michel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrick_Michel)

The internal structure of asteroids is inferred only from indirect evidence: bulk densities measured by spacecraft, the orbits of natural satellites in the case of asteroid binaries, and the drift of an asteroid's orbit due to the Yarkovsky thermal effect. A spacecraft near an asteroid is perturbed enough by the asteroid's gravity to allow an estimate of the asteroid's mass. The volume is then estimated using a model of the asteroid's shape. Mass and volume allow the derivation of the bulk density, whose uncertainty is usually dominated by the errors made on the volume estimate. The internal porosity of asteroids can be inferred by comparing their bulk density with that of their assumed meteorite analogues, dark asteroids seem to be more porous (>40%) than bright ones. The nature of this porosity is unclear.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-michel-146)

Dedicated missions

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=33)]

The first asteroid to be photographed in close-up was [951 Gaspra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/951_Gaspra) in 1991, followed in 1993 by [243 Ida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/243_Ida) and its moon [Dactyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dactyl_(asteroid)), all of which were imaged by the [Galileo probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)) en route to [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter). Other asteroids briefly visited by spacecraft en route to other destinations include [9969 Braille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9969_Braille) (by [Deep Space 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_1) in 1999), [5535 Annefrank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5535_Annefrank) (by [Stardust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)) in 2002), [2867 Šteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2867_Šteins) and [21 Lutetia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21_Lutetia) (by the [Rosetta probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft)) in 2008), and [4179 Toutatis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4179_Toutatis) (China's lunar orbiter [Chang'e 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chang'e_2), which flew within 3.2 km (2 mi) in 2012).

The first dedicated asteroid probe was NASA's [NEAR Shoemaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NEAR_Shoemaker), which photographed [253 Mathilde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/253_Mathilde) in 1997, before entering into orbit around [433 Eros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/433_Eros), finally landing on its surface in 2001. It was the first spacecraft to successfully orbit and land on an asteroid.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-twofirsts-153) From September to November 2005, the Japanese [Hayabusa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayabusa_(spacecraft)) probe studied [25143 Itokawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/25143_Itokawa) in detail and [returned samples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sample_return_mission) of its surface to Earth on 13 June 2010, the first asteroid sample-return mission. In 2007, [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA) launched the [Dawn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawn_(spacecraft)) spacecraft, which orbited [4 Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta) for a year, and observed the dwarf planet [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)) for three years.

[Hayabusa2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayabusa2), a probe launched by [JAXA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JAXA) 2014, orbited its target asteroid [162173 Ryugu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/162173_Ryugu) for more than a year and took samples that were delivered to Earth in 2020. The spacecraft is now on an extended mission and expected to arrive at a new target in 2031.

NASA launched the [OSIRIS-REx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OSIRIS-REx) in 2016, a sample return mission to asteroid [101955 Bennu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/101955_Bennu). In 2021, the probe departed the asteroid with a sample from its surface. Sample was delivered to Earth in September 2023. The spacecraft continues its extended mission, designated OSIRIS-APEX, to explore near-Earth asteroid Apophis in 2029.

In 2021, NASA launched [Double Asteroid Redirection Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Asteroid_Redirection_Test) (DART), a mission to test technology for defending Earth against potential hazardous objects. DART deliberately crashed into the [minor-planet moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor-planet_moon)[Dimorphos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimorphos) of the double asteroid [Didymos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/65803_Didymos) in September 2022 to assess the potential of a spacecraft impact to deflect an asteroid from a collision course with Earth.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-154) In October, NASA declared DART a success, confirming it had shortened Dimorphos's orbital period around Didymos by about 32 minutes.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-155)

NASA's [Lucy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucy_(spacecraft)), launched in 2021, is a multiple-asteroid flyby probe focused on flying by 7 [Jupiter trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan) of varying types. While not yet set to reach its first main target, [3548 Eurybates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3548_Eurybates), until 2027, it has made flybys of the main-belt asteroids [152830 Dinkinesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/152830_Dinkinesh) and [52246 Donaldjohanson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/52246_Donaldjohanson).[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-156)[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-157)

NASA's [Psyche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psyche_(spacecraft)), launched in October 2023, is intended to study the large metallic asteroid [of the same name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16_Psyche), and is on track to arrive there in 2029.

ESA's [Hera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hera_(space_mission)), launched in October 2024, is intended study the results of the DART impact. It is expected to measure the size and morphology of the crater, and momentum transmitted by the impact, to determine the efficiency of the deflection produced by DART.

CNSA's [Tianwen-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianwen-2) was launched in May 2025.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-auto-6) It will use [solar electric propulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_electric_propulsion) to explore the [co-orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital) near-Earth asteroid [469219 Kamoʻoalewa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/469219_Kamoʻoalewa) and the [active asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_asteroid)[311P/PanSTARRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/311P/PanSTARRS). The spacecraft is tasked with collecting samples of the regolith of Kamo'oalewa.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nature20190430-7)

Asteroid mining

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=35)]



The concept of asteroid mining was proposed in the 1970s. Matt Anderson defines successful asteroid mining as "the development of a mining program that is both financially self-sustaining and profitable to its investors".[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-159) It has been suggested that asteroids might be used as a source of materials that may be rare or exhausted on Earth,[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-160) or materials for constructing [space habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_habitat). Materials that are heavy and expensive to launch from Earth may someday be mined from asteroids and used for [space manufacturing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_manufacturing) and construction.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-161)[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-162)

As [resource depletion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_depletion) on Earth becomes more real, the idea of extracting valuable elements from asteroids and returning these to [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) for profit, or using space-based resources to build [solar-power satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_solar_power) and [space habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_habitats),[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-163)[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-164) becomes more attractive. Hypothetically, water processed from ice could refuel orbiting [propellant depots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propellant_depot).[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-165)[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Kiss-166)

From the [astrobiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology) perspective, asteroid prospecting could provide scientific data for the search for extraterrestrial intelligence ([SETI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SETI)). Some astrophysicists have suggested that if advanced extraterrestrial civilizations employed asteroid mining long ago, the hallmarks of these activities might be detectable.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-167)[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-168)[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-169)

Threats to Earth

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=36)]



[bolides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolide), small asteroids roughly 1 to 20 meters in diameter impacting Earth's atmosphere

There is increasing interest in identifying asteroids whose orbits cross [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth)'s, and that could, given enough time, collide with Earth. The three most important groups of [near-Earth asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroid) are the [Apollos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_asteroid), [Amors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amor_asteroid), and [Atens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aten_asteroid).

The [near-Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_object) asteroid [433 Eros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/433_Eros) had been discovered as long ago as 1898, and the 1930s brought a flurry of similar objects. In order of discovery, these were: [1221 Amor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1221_Amor), [1862 Apollo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1862_Apollo), [2101 Adonis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2101_Adonis), and finally [69230 Hermes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/69230_Hermes), which approached within 0.005 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit) of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) in 1937. Astronomers began to realize the possibilities of Earth impact.

Two events in later decades increased the alarm: the increasing acceptance of the [Alvarez hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alvarez_hypothesis) that an [impact event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_event) resulted in the [Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous–Paleogene_extinction_event), and the 1994 observation of [Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Shoemaker-Levy_9) crashing into [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_events_on_Jupiter). The U.S. military also declassified the information that its [military satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_satellite), built to [detect nuclear explosions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detect_nuclear_explosions), had detected hundreds of upper-atmosphere impacts by objects ranging from one to ten meters across.

All of these considerations helped spur the launch of highly efficient surveys, consisting of charge-coupled device ([CCD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-coupled_device)) cameras and computers directly connected to telescopes. As of 2011[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit), it was estimated that 89% to 96% of near-Earth asteroids one kilometer or larger in diameter had been discovered.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-nasa_neo-60) As of 29 October 2018[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit), the LINEAR system alone had discovered 147,132 asteroids.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-170) Among the surveys, 19,266 near-Earth asteroids have been discovered[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-171) including almost 900 more than 1 km (0.6 mi) in diameter.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-172)

In June 2018, the [National Science and Technology Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Science_and_Technology_Council) warned that the United States is unprepared for an asteroid impact event, and has developed and released the "National Near-Earth Object Preparedness Strategy Action Plan" to better prepare.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-GIZ-20180621-173)[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-ICARUS-220180522-174)[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-NYT-20180614-175) According to expert testimony in the [United States Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Congress) in 2013, [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA) would require at least five years of preparation before a mission to intercept an asteroid could be launched.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-US-Congress-20130410-176)

Asteroid deflection strategies

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=37)]



[Double Asteroid Redirection Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Asteroid_Redirection_Test)in 2022 demonstrated that spacecraft impact is a viable option for

[planetary defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_impact_avoidance).

Various collision avoidance techniques have different trade-offs with respect to metrics such as overall performance, cost, failure risks, operations, and technology readiness.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-177) There are various methods for changing the course of an asteroid/comet.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HallRoss-178) These can be differentiated by various types of attributes such as the type of mitigation (deflection or fragmentation), energy source (kinetic, electromagnetic, gravitational, solar/thermal, or nuclear), and approach strategy (interception,[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-179)[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-180) rendezvous, or remote station).

Strategies fall into two basic sets: fragmentation and delay.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-HallRoss-178)[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-181) Fragmentation concentrates on rendering the impactor harmless by fragmenting it and scattering the fragments so that they miss the Earth or are small enough to burn up in the atmosphere. Delay exploits the fact that both the Earth and the impactor are in orbit. An impact occurs when both reach the same point in space at the same time, or more correctly when some point on Earth's surface intersects the impactor's orbit when the impactor arrives. Since the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) is approximately 12,750 km (7,920 mi) in diameter and moves at approx. 30 km/s (19 mi/s) in its orbit, it travels a distance of one planetary diameter in about 425 seconds, or slightly over seven minutes. Delaying, or advancing the impactor's arrival by times of this magnitude can, depending on the exact geometry of the impact, cause it to miss the Earth.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-RossParkPorter-182)

"[Project Icarus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1566_Icarus#Project_Icarus)" was one of the first projects designed in 1967 as a contingency plan in case of collision with [1566 Icarus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1566_Icarus). The plan relied on the new [Saturn V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn_V) rocket, which did not make its first flight until after the report had been completed. Six Saturn V rockets would be used, each launched at variable intervals from months to hours away from impact. Each rocket was to be fitted with a single 100-megaton [nuclear warhead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_warhead) as well as a modified [Apollo Service Module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_Service_Module) and uncrewed [Apollo Command Module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_Command_Module) for guidance to the target. The warheads would be detonated 30 meters from the surface, deflecting or partially destroying the asteroid. Depending on the subsequent impacts on the course or the destruction of the asteroid, later missions would be modified or cancelled as needed. The "last-ditch" launch of the sixth rocket would be 18 hours prior to impact.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-Portree-183)

Fiction

[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asteroid&action=edit&section=38)]

Asteroids and the asteroid belt are a staple of science fiction stories. Asteroids play several potential roles in science fiction: as places human beings might colonize, resources for extracting minerals, hazards encountered by spacecraft traveling between two other points, and as a threat to life on Earth or other inhabited planets, dwarf planets, and natural satellites by potential impact.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org#cite_note-184)
